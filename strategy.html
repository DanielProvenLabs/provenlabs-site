Phase 1 — Selection

Why these five industries.

Friends in the industry
🏗️ Contractors — Priority #1
I have friends in contracting who talk about this regularly. The work gets done — the business side doesn't. Invoices don't go out on time, follow-ups fall through, and keeping it all straight becomes its own full-time job nobody signed up for. I've heard this enough times that I know exactly what to ask when I walk in the door.
Family in the industry
🏠 Real estate — Priority #2
I have family in real estate. What I've seen up close is the paperwork and compliance burden — contracts, disclosures, the NAR settlement adding more — combined with the scheduling chaos that comes with running an active book of clients. The systems aren't keeping up with the workload and I've watched that play out firsthand.
Personal experience as a customer
🔧 Auto repair — Priority #3
I switched mechanics because I found a cheaper option. My original shop never called, never followed up, never gave me a reason to stay. No reminder, no check-in, nothing. I didn't leave because the work was bad — I left because they made it easy to leave. That's a retention problem I experienced directly, and it's fixable.
Market data + career interest
⚖️ Law firms — Priority #4
No personal connection, but two honest reasons: the data is too strong to ignore — a $405B industry where 60% of firms never answer email and 7% actually use their CRM — and I've genuinely considered law as a career path, which means I've thought about how the profession works from the inside. That gives me more context than pure numbers alone.
Friend in the profession
📊 Accounting — Priority #5
I have a friend who works in accounting. Her description of the job is blunt: a lot of repeatable, boring work. That's not a personal failing — it's a systems problem. The manual, repetitive work that fills her days is exactly what AI is built to eliminate, and 79% of firm owners already know it's coming. Almost none of them have a plan for it.

Five industries. Deep research.

01 🏗️ Contractors & construction
Observed gap Priority #1
Industry profile

The largest of the five industries and the most operationally primitive. Construction is 4.4% of US GDP, employs 8.3 million people, and is dominated by small owner-operators who are tradespeople first and business owners second. Most contractors manage projects from memory, run quotes in their heads, and invoice late if at all. The gap between the quality of the work and the quality of the administration is enormous — and visible to anyone paying attention.

02 🏠 Real estate agencies & brokerages
Personal connection Priority #2
Industry profile

A fragmented, relationship-driven industry where the largest player holds only 9% market share. 81% of brokerage firms operate a single office. The NAR settlement in 2024 added new disclosure requirements and liability pressure, accelerating the shift toward flat-fee and rebate models. Existing home sales remain near 30-year lows as homeowners hold sub-4% mortgages — deals are harder to come by, making conversion and retention more critical than ever.

03 🔧 Automotive repair shops
Personal experience Priority #3
Industry profile

A highly fragmented industry where independent owner-operators generate the majority of revenue, but operate on legacy tools or nothing at all. The average US vehicle is now 12.8 years old — older vehicles mean more frequent repairs and higher ticket sizes, but shops aren't built to capitalize on retention. The trust gap is the defining characteristic: two-thirds of Americans don't trust their repair shop, which means the bar for winning loyalty is low if you simply show up reliably.

Outreach Tactics.

01 Outreach tactics
🏗️ Contractors
In personShow up at a job site or their shop in the morning before the day gets away from them. Best entry by far — these owners are never at a desk.
Phone callDirect call to the business number. Short, no pitch — just asking for 15 minutes. Second best to showing up in person.
EmailFollow-up only — never a cold opener. Use it to send a recap or confirm a next step after a real conversation.
🏠 Real estate
Warm introOne person vouching for you through family connections is worth 50 cold emails. Start here before anything else.
Phone callBrokers are on their phones constantly. A direct call to the office works better than most expect — they're used to fielding calls.
LinkedInWorks well for follow-up after a call. Not a cold opener — use it to stay visible after the first touch.
🔧 Automotive repair
In personWalk in mid-morning after the rush — not first thing, not at lunch. Shop owners are on the floor, not checking email. Easiest in-person approach of the five.
Phone callCall the shop directly if you can't get there in person. Keep it short and make it about them — ask what's slowing them down, not what you're selling.
EmailFollow-up only. Useful for sending a summary after a conversation, not for cold outreach.
⚖️ Law firms
EmailA short, well-written email referencing a specific pain point gets read. Cold calls get screened by the receptionist — email bypasses that.
Phone callFollow up with a call after the email — reference it directly. Don't cold call blind; warm the line first.
LinkedInWorks especially well with younger attorneys. Use it in parallel with email to stay on their radar.
📊 Accounting
EmailA thoughtful email referencing tax season overwhelm or the too-many-tools problem will outperform a cold call. Desk-based buyers read their inbox.
LinkedInFirm owners are more active here than attorneys tend to be. Strong for both cold outreach and follow-up — personalize every message.
Phone callOnly after email or LinkedIn has warmed the line. These buyers are time-protective — a cold call without context rarely lands.
02 Messaging & value proposition
General opener — all industries
ProvenLabs leads with diagnosis before prescription because recommending a solution before understanding the problem is how bad consultants operate. A business owner who feels heard — not sold to — is more likely to tell you the truth about what's actually broken, which is the only way to build something that actually works for them. The goal of the first conversation is never to close. It's to earn the right to come back with a real recommendation.
"I'm not here to sell you anything on this call. I want to understand your operation — then I'll tell you honestly what I think would help, and what I think is a waste of your time."
That's the opener because it's true and because it disarms. Most business owners have been pitched at so many times that honesty is the differentiator.
Industry-specific hooks
🏗️ Contractors
"Most small contractors I talk to are great at the work but drowning in the business side of it — I'm not sure if this sounds familiar, but 70% of contractors experience payment delays beyond 30 days and only 18% are using anything beyond email or spreadsheets. What I do is come in, learn your operation, and build you a system that gets you paid faster and keeps jobs from falling through the cracks — and whatever else we find once I'm actually in the room with you."
🏠 Real estate
"A lot of brokers I speak with feel like they're working hard but losing deals they should have closed — I'm not sure if this sounds familiar, but 48% of agents never follow up after first contact and 60% of buyers say no four times before they say yes. What I do is diagnose exactly where your follow-up is breaking down and put a system in place that keeps you in front of leads automatically — along with anything else that's quietly costing you money."
🔧 Automotive repair
"Most shop owners I talk to know they have regulars who just stopped coming in, but have no real way to know why — I'm not sure if this sounds familiar, but two-thirds of Americans say they don't trust their repair shop and most shops have no system to bring customers back. What I do is build a retention and follow-up system that keeps your existing customers coming back — and take a look at what else in your operation could be running cleaner."
⚖️ Law firms
"A lot of small firm attorneys I speak with are spending half their day on things that have nothing to do with practicing law — I'm not sure if this sounds familiar, but 45% of small firm time goes to non-billable admin, adding up to over $100,000 in lost revenue per attorney per year. What I do is identify where that time is going and build intake and workflow systems that give it back to you — and surface whatever else is slowing the practice down."
📊 Accounting
"Most firm owners I talk to feel like they're constantly reacting — too many tools, not enough time, growth entirely dependent on word of mouth — I'm not sure if this sounds familiar, but the average accounting firm runs eight disconnected apps and two-thirds feel overwhelmed by technology at least weekly. What I do is simplify the stack and build systems that free you up to focus on advisory work — and figure out what else is getting in the way of growing the firm."

How I used AI to build this.

Claude was my analyst, copywriter, researcher, and sparring partner at every phase. Here's exactly how — not a vague claim, but a specific accounting of the work.

Research
Used Claude to run industry profiles, pain maps, ICP definitions, market sizing, and software landscape analyses for all five industries. I directed the research and challenged the output — pushed back on vague statistics, asked for verification, and separated what was confirmed from what was estimated.
Prioritization
Used Claude as a sparring partner to stress-test my prioritization logic. It pushed back on "good upside" as a reason — and it was right. That challenge forced me to separate personal-connection picks from data-driven ones, which made the plan more honest.
Messaging
Drafted and iterated the value proposition and industry-specific hooks with Claude. Went through multiple rounds — the first drafts were generic. The current version came from pushing past the obvious framing and asking "what does this owner actually need to hear?"
Design & build
This site was built with Claude. Claude interviewed me before writing a line of code — the architecture, the phase structure, and the content all came from that conversation. The design choices were deliberate, not defaults.
Honesty check
Claude flagged where my reasoning was thin — specifically on law firms and accounting, where "good upside" was not a real reason. I took the feedback and said so explicitly in the plan rather than papering over it. That's on the site because it's true.
